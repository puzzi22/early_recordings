Édouard Vuillermoz

Biography

Édouard Vuillermoz (or Louis Édouard Vuillermoz or Louis E. Vuillermoz) (born on February 13, 1869 in Quingey and died on February 28, 1939 in Paris) was a horn player and music teacher from France.

Édouard Vuillermoz studies at the Paris Conservatory in the class of Jean Mohr (1823-1891) and receives the first prize on natural horn in 1889. From 1894 he becomes a member of the Orchestre de la Société des Concerts du Conservatoire. He then becomes solo horn at the Concerts du Châtelet and with the Paris Opera. He then plays a piston horn. After 15 years in the France capital, Édouard Vuillermoz moves to Monte Carlo in 1904 to play in the orchestra of the Casino which is conducted by Léon Jehin.

At a concert given in Berlin in 1906 he plays the first horn part to Robert Schumann's Konzertstück Op. 86 with the Berlin Philharmonie.

John Humphries, is his book The Early Horn, says that he cultivated a gentle, almost completely straight, sound.

Igor Stravinsky consults with him about the playing technique that consists of raising the bell of the instrument in the air, while composing the Rite of Spring in 1910.

In September 1925, he is offered the position of solo horn of the Société des Concerts in Paris, with whom he plays as a soloist Paul Dukas' Villanelle. He also played in the orchestra of the Straram Concerts, which was created in 1925 and comprising the best musicians from the parisian orchestras of the time. He was also a member of the Société du Double Quintette de Paris. He taught at the Paris Conservatory for 3 years only from 1934 to 1937.

Édouard Vuillermoz composed Dix pièces mélodiques. He also wrote several pedagogical works, as well as a series of transcriptions for horn. At the beginning of the 1920's, Jean-Marie Maxime-Alphonse (then solo horn of the Opéra-Comique and Concerts-Pasdeloup) chooses Vuillermoz as dedicatee of the Six cahiers d'études nouvelles et progressives pour cor, still in use nowadays.

He was the father of composer Jean Vuillermoz (1906-1940), born in Monte Carlo, and horn player Louis Vuillermoz (1898-1953). In the 1930s he performed regularly with his son Louis for concerts and on the radio.

He died in Paris on 28 February, 1939.

Instrument Design

Édouard Vuillermoz collaborated with French brass instrument makers, and had an important influence of horn playing techniques, most notably by adopting the double horn with an ascending third valve, a compensating instrument in Bb/F (C/G).

This type of instrument was perfected in France by his son, Louis Vuillermoz (1898-1953), who collaborated with Jérôme Thibouville-Lamy around 1925.

The model is improved in the 1930's by Henri Selmer.

« Mon fils Louis est le promoteur en France du cor double en fa et en si bémol ou en ut avec troisième piston descendant ou ascendant, qui combine toutes les gammes de notes graves et aiguës et dont l'utilisation pratique ne bouleverse en rien la technique du cor. Cet instrument est capable de rendre les plus grands services aux cornistes - je l'ai moi-même adopté, et il sera sûrement utilisé exclusivement pendant quelque temps... »

— Édouard Vuillermoz
