Project Description

In 1877 Thomas Edison invented the first device able to record and reproduce sound: the phonograph. Even though the original invention was designed to record the humain voice, Edison realized the immense potential of recording music, and he set off to visit several of the world's capitals with the dual goal of promoting his invention and recording some of the foremost artists of his time. Jean-Baptiste Arban, the famous cornet player, and flautist Paul Taffanel both participated in Edison's first recording sessions. Unfortunately, these rolls were never found again.

The benefits of studying historical recordings are numerous:

  • Understand national differences in styles and techniques
  • Get closer to the playing styles composers from the era knew and expected
  • Understand the playing caracteristics of 19th-century trained horn players
  • Find inspiration

There are relatively few recordings featuring the horn as a soloist in the first few decades of the 20th century. A few notable ones:

  • 1905: Titl's Sérénade by Arthur Delgrange (1858-1932)
  • 1909: Rossini's Quartet No. 1 by anonymous (American?) musicians
  • 1910: Mendelssohn's Der Jäger Abschied by Karl Stiegler (1876-1932) leading the quartet
  • 1912: Wagner's Siegfried Horn Call by Anton Horner (1877-1971)
  • 1912: Saint-Saëns' Romance en fa, op. 36 by Anton Horner
  • 1929: Émile Vuillermoz's Piece Mélodique by Édouard Vuillermoz (1869-1939)
  • 1929: Saint-Saëns' Romance en fa, op. 36 by Édouard Vuillermoz (excerpt)
